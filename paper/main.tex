\begin{abstract}
Fast charging of lithium-ion battery packs requires simultaneous control of charging speed, safety, and degradation under model uncertainty, sensor noise, and cell-to-cell heterogeneity. This paper presents Safe Adaptive Ensemble Reinforcement Learning (SAERL), a hybrid charging framework that combines three complementary one-step predictors (GRU, MLP, and random forest) through adaptive uncertainty-aware gating, learns a residual policy over a model predictive control (MPC) anchor, and enforces hard voltage, temperature, current, and state-of-charge limits through a safety shield with explicit fallback actions. SAERL is evaluated on 27 realistic charging scenarios constructed from NASA, CALCE, and MATR data across fastest, safe, and long-life objectives, using a pack-level electro-thermal-aging model with explicit heterogeneity, state-estimation error, and sensor noise. Among all compared controllers, including CCCV, MPC, PPO-only, and static-ensemble baselines, SAERL is the only method that achieves non-zero benchmark acceptance, passing 7/27 scenarios (25.9\%). It records zero safety failures and zero temperature failures, passes the q-loss criterion in 24/27 cases (88.9\%), and reaches a mean time to 80\% SoC of 73.3\,min with a mean inference latency of 28.7\,ms, which is compatible with real-time battery-management operation. Relative to CCCV, SAERL reaches 80\% SoC earlier while eliminating hundreds of safety events on average; relative to MPC, it removes all safety events and slightly improves final SoC and q-loss, but remains slower on aggregate. The results show that SAERL is already a safety-dominant and practically deployable hybrid charging architecture, while also making clear that its main remaining limitation is target-reaching performance under domain shift, especially in the NASA aging regime where training support and predictive fidelity are weakest.

 
\end{abstract}

\section{\label{sec_introduction}Introduction}

Rapid and safe recharge is a central requirement for electrified transport and stationary storage. Lithium ion cells offer high energy density, but they operate within narrow voltage, current, and temperature limits. Violating these limits accelerates degradation and can initiate unsafe failure pathways, including thermal runaway \cite{hu2020,tomaszewska2019}. Fast charging therefore naturally forms a constrained control problem that aims to reduce charge time while respecting safety limits and limiting degradation.

Industrial chargers still rely largely on constant current constant voltage (CCCV) because it is simple, robust, and comparatively easy to certify \cite{tomaszewska2019}. In CCCV, a fixed current is applied until a voltage threshold is reached, then the charger holds voltage while the current tapers. This open loop structure is typically conservative, and it cannot explicitly adapt to temperature variation, parameter drift with aging, or cell to cell spread within a pack. These effects become dominant at high C rates, where polarization and heat generation rise sharply \cite{tanim2018}. In addition, pack level performance can be limited by the most stressed cell, since cell variability in resistance and capacity drives unequal currents, unequal heating, and unequal aging \cite{xin2024machines}.

Model predictive control (MPC) is an attractive alternative because it optimizes a finite horizon under explicit constraints using a physics based model. However, two issues limit pack level deployment. First, pack safety is fundamentally cell constrained. For a series string with $N$ cells, enforcing per cell voltage and temperature bounds yields at least $2N$ hard constraints per time step and $2NH$ constraints over a horizon of length $H$. If the controller also tracks per cell electrochemical and thermal states, then both the decision dimension and the constraint set scale with $N$, and the resulting quadratic program grows quickly in size. This makes real time operation harder as pack size grows, unless substantial simplifications or decentralization are introduced. Second, MPC performance and constraint satisfaction depend on model fidelity. Parameter drift due to aging and temperature, together with cell heterogeneity, can create mismatch that erodes safety margin unless the model is updated online \cite{liu2018}.

Reinforcement learning (RL) can learn charging policies that exploit nonlinearities and adapt from data, and recent work shows that adding explicit safety mechanisms can reduce violations during learning and deployment \cite{wang2023saclagrange}. Yet standard model free RL still faces two practical barriers for batteries. First, it typically requires many interactions, which is expensive or infeasible on physical cells. Second, unsafe exploratory actions can violate safety constraints during training, wasting episodes and risking damage. This motivates hybrid designs that keep learning within safe envelopes while still enabling performance gains beyond classical baselines.

We formulate pack fast charging as a safety critical control problem with hard constraints enforced at the cell level. For all times $t$ and each cell $i \in \{1,\ldots,N\}$, we require
\begin{equation}
V_i(t) \in [V_{\min}, V_{\max}],
\qquad
T_i(t) \in [T_{\min}, T_{\max}],
\label{eq_cell_safety}
\end{equation}
together with pack level limits on current and total voltage. Safety monitoring and termination use worst case quantities, including $\max_i V_i(t)$ and $\max_i T_i(t)$, rather than pack averages, so that safety cannot be masked by averaging. This per cell formulation is consistent with the practical reality that the first cell to hit a voltage or temperature limit determines the allowable pack current, especially in the presence of heterogeneity \cite{xin2024machines}.

We propose SAERL, Safe Adaptive Ensemble Reinforcement Learning, a hybrid framework for pack level fast charging under strict per cell safety limits. The main contributions are

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Uncertainty aware ensemble adaptation for charging prediction}
SAERL integrates complementary predictors, including quantile based neural models and a variance aware tree model, and reweights them online using rolling prediction error and predictive uncertainty. This improves robustness under sensor noise, parameter drift, and cell to cell variability.

\item \textbf{Residual learning over a constrained baseline with a differentiable safety shield}
SAERL learns a residual correction on top of a baseline constrained controller to accelerate learning while keeping exploration near a safe operating region. Candidate actions pass through a differentiable safety shield that checks per cell hard constraints on voltage and temperature, plus operational bounds on current and state of charge, before application. Unsafe actions are rejected before the environment is stepped.

\item \textbf{Pack level evaluation under heterogeneity and worst cell metrics}
We evaluate SAERL on a pack model with explicit cell to cell variation in capacity and resistance. Performance is reported using worst cell voltage and temperature trajectories, enabling direct analysis of imbalance and safety margins that single cell studies cannot capture \cite{xin2024machines}.

\item \textbf{Cross regime scenario evaluation with public cycling data}
We construct fast charging scenarios from public sources that span different ambient conditions and cycling regimes, including the NASA prognostics repository data \cite{saha2008} and the MATR related cycle life study \cite{severson2019}. To avoid ambiguous mixing, all scenarios are mapped to a common cell model through parameter identification, ensuring consistent state definitions and constraint thresholds across domains.
\end{enumerate}

\section{\label{sec:related-work}Background and Related Work}

\subsection{\label{subsec:charging-strategies}Charging Strategies}

\subsubsection{\label{subsubsec:cccv}Constant Current Constant Voltage (CCCV)}

Constant current constant voltage (CCCV) remains the industrial benchmark for lithium ion battery charging because it is simple, reliable, and straightforward to implement and certify \cite{tomaszewska2019lithium,waldmann2014optimization}. CCCV consists of two phases. In the constant current (CC) phase, the charger applies a fixed current $I_{\mathrm{cc}}$ until the terminal voltage reaches the upper cutoff $V_{\max}$. In the constant voltage (CV) phase, the charger holds $V(t)=V_{\max}$ while the current naturally tapers, and charging terminates when $I(t)$ falls below a threshold $I_{\mathrm{term}}$ such as C/20 \cite{liu2018charging}.

A compact description is
\begin{equation}
I(t) = I_{\mathrm{cc}}, \quad \text{while } V(t) < V_{\max}.
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
V(t) = V_{\max}, \quad \text{while } I(t) > I_{\mathrm{term}}.
\end{equation}

During the CV phase, the current taper can be related to the difference between the imposed terminal voltage and the evolving open circuit voltage. Under a simplified resistance dominated approximation,
\begin{equation}
I(t) \approx \frac{V_{\max} - V_{\mathrm{oc}}(t)}{R_0(t)} .
\end{equation}
This explains the well known long tail near end of charge as $V_{\mathrm{oc}}(t)$ approaches $V_{\max}$ and as $R_0(t)$ increases with aging and low temperature \cite{tomaszewska2019lithium,waldmann2014optimization}.

CCCV has clear practical advantages. It is open loop and does not require explicit estimation of internal states such as state of charge (SoC) or state of health (SoH), and it does not depend on identifying or updating model parameters online. This makes it robust across cell formats and operating conditions, especially in low cost charger implementations.

However, CCCV is not time optimal for fast charging. High $I_{\mathrm{cc}}$ often drives the terminal voltage to $V_{\max}$ early due to polarization, forcing an extended CV phase where the delivered charge per unit time steadily decreases. As a result, increasing $I_{\mathrm{cc}}$ yields diminishing returns in total charge time \cite{tomaszewska2019lithium}. More importantly, the same fixed $(I_{\mathrm{cc}},V_{\max})$ pair can be benign for a fresh warm cell yet risky for a cold or aged cell, since higher resistance and slower diffusion amplify overpotential and local lithium plating propensity at high rates \cite{waldmann2014optimization,tomaszewska2019lithium}. These limitations motivate baseline alternatives such as multistage and pulse charging, which seek to reduce the CV tail and mitigate stress, though they still rely on rule design and may remain chemistry and condition dependent \cite{beh2019pulse,tomaszewska2019lithium}.

\subsubsection{\label{subsubsec:mpc}Model Predictive Control (MPC)}

Model predictive control (MPC) replaces fixed rules with repeated finite horizon optimization under an explicit battery model. At each sampling instant $k$, MPC computes a current sequence that minimizes a cost while enforcing safety limits over a horizon of length $H$
\begin{equation}
\min_{\{I_{k+j}\}_{j=0}^{H-1}}
\sum_{j=0}^{H-1} \ell(x_{k+j}, I_{k+j}) + \Phi(x_{k+H}).
\end{equation}
subject to
\begin{equation}
x_{k+j+1} = f(x_{k+j}, I_{k+j}), \quad j=0,\ldots,H-1,
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
V_{\min} \le V_{k+j} \le V_{\max}, \quad j=0,\ldots,H-1,
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
T_{\min} \le T_{k+j} \le T_{\max}, \quad j=0,\ldots,H-1,
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
0 \le I_{k+j} \le I_{\max}, \quad j=0,\ldots,H-1.
\end{equation}
Here $x_k$ contains the states used by the embedded model, for example SoC, polarization states, and thermal states, and $\ell(\cdot)$ can penalize time, current stress, and degradation related surrogates \cite{liu2018charging,krewer2018review}.

The main advantage of MPC is systematic constraint handling. Voltage, temperature, and current bounds can be embedded as hard constraints, and the predictive structure allows the controller to preemptively reduce current before violating limits rather than reacting after the fact.

Pack level deployment introduces two recurring issues. First, the optimization size grows with pack granularity. If a series string has $N$ cells and the safety specification is per cell, then enforcing bounds on each $V_i$ and $T_i$ yields at least $2N$ inequality constraints per step and $2NH$ constraints over the horizon. If the model tracks per cell polarization and thermal dynamics, the state dimension also increases with $N$, increasing solve times and making real time implementation harder as pack size grows \cite{pinter2025reconfigurable,krewer2018review}. This scaling argument can be summarized as
\begin{equation}
m_{\mathrm{ineq}}(N,H) \ge 2NH,
\end{equation}
where $m_{\mathrm{ineq}}$ is the number of per step inequality constraints aggregated over the horizon.

Second, MPC performance depends on model fidelity. Equivalent circuit and reduced order electrochemical models are approximations and their parameters drift with temperature and aging. Model mismatch can be written as
\begin{equation}
x_{k+1} = f_{\mathrm{true}}(x_k, I_k) = f_{\mathrm{model}}(x_k, I_k) + w_k,
\end{equation}
where $w_k$ captures unmodeled dynamics and parameter errors. In charging, even modest errors in internal resistance can shift predicted terminal voltage and shrink the effective safety margin. For example, if $R_0$ is overestimated, then the predicted terminal voltage
\begin{equation}
\widehat{V}_t = V_{\mathrm{oc}} - \widehat{R}_0 I
\end{equation}
suggests earlier saturation and drives unnecessary current derating, increasing charge time \cite{liu2018charging,krewer2018review}. These limitations motivate hybrid approaches that retain MPC as a safe baseline while learning residual corrections and uncertainty handling mechanisms, aligned with the proposed method in this paper.

\subsection{Reinforcement Learning for Battery Control}

Reinforcement learning (RL) offers a data driven route to learn charging policies directly from interaction, without requiring an explicit closed form electrochemical model. Fast charging can be posed as a Markov decision process (MDP) with state $s_t \in \mathcal{S}$, action $a_t \in \mathcal{A}$, transition kernel $P(s_{t+1}\mid s_t,a_t)$, reward $r(s_t,a_t)$, and discount factor $\gamma \in (0,1]$. In battery charging, the state commonly aggregates measurable or estimated quantities such as SoC, terminal voltage, temperature, and short histories of these signals, while the action is typically the commanded charge current or an incremental adjustment to a baseline current.

\subsubsection{Model free reinforcement learning}
Model free RL methods learn a policy $\pi_\theta(a\mid s)$ directly from reward signals. The standard objective maximizes the expected discounted return
\begin{equation}
J(\pi_\theta)
=
\mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi_\theta}
\left[
\sum_{t=0}^{T}
\gamma^{t}\, r(s_t,a_t)
\right],
\label{eq_return_objective}
\end{equation}
where $\tau=(s_0,a_0,s_1,a_1,\dots)$ is a trajectory induced by the policy. Value based approaches such as deep Q networks and policy gradient methods such as PPO have been used in battery energy management and charging control settings \cite{liu2019brief,wu2020battery,schulman2017proximal}. For PPO, policy updates are commonly computed by maximizing the clipped surrogate objective
\begin{equation}
L_{\text{CLIP}}(\theta)
=
\mathbb{E}_{t}
\left[
\min
\left(
\rho_t(\theta)\,\hat{A}_t,
\text{clip}\!\left(\rho_t(\theta),1-\epsilon,1+\epsilon\right)\hat{A}_t
\right)
\right],
\label{eq_ppo_clip}
\end{equation}
with probability ratio
\begin{equation}
\rho_t(\theta)
=
\frac{\pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(a_t\mid s_t)},
\label{eq_ppo_ratio}
\end{equation}
advantage estimate $\hat{A}_t$, and clipping hyperparameter $\epsilon$ \cite{schulman2017proximal}. Despite flexibility, model free RL remains difficult to apply on physical cells because learning often requires very large numbers of episodes, while each charging episode is time consuming and contributes measurable aging. In addition, unconstrained exploration can violate voltage and temperature limits, which is unacceptable for safety critical battery operation.

\subsubsection{Model based reinforcement learning}
Model based RL improves sample efficiency by learning a predictive dynamics model and using it for planning or policy optimization. A common one step stochastic predictor is
\begin{equation}
\hat{s}_{t+1}
=
f_{\phi}(s_t,a_t) + \varepsilon_t,
\qquad
\varepsilon_t \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\Sigma_{\phi}(s_t,a_t)\right),
\label{eq_model_based_dynamics}
\end{equation}
where $f_\phi$ is a learned function approximator and $\Sigma_\phi$ captures aleatoric uncertainty. Planning with learned probabilistic dynamics and short horizon rollouts has been shown to reduce real interaction requirements in general control tasks \cite{chua2018deep}. For batteries, recent work also explores deep RL charging strategies that incorporate aging models directly in the learning loop, highlighting both the promise and the difficulty of achieving robust, transferable policies across conditions \cite{yadu2024personalized}. The key risk is model bias, where prediction errors induce unsafe actions or overly aggressive currents when deployed outside the training distribution.

\subsubsection{Safe reinforcement learning with safety layers and shielding}
Safety constrained charging can be written using inequality constraints $g(s_t,a_t)\le 0$ that encode voltage, temperature, current, and SoC limits. A widely used safety layer is action projection, which modifies the RL proposed action $a_t^{\text{RL}}$ by solving
\begin{equation}
a_t^{\text{safe}}
=
\arg\min_{a \in \mathcal{A}}
\left\|a-a_t^{\text{RL}}\right\|_2^2
\quad
\text{subject to}
\quad
g(s_t,a)\le 0.
\label{eq_safe_projection}
\end{equation}
For battery fast charging, Wei et al. applied this idea with linearized constraints and quadratic programming, improving safety while adding per step optimization overhead \cite{wei2021safe}. Pack level deployment can further increase cost since per cell voltage and temperature limits yield constraint sets that grow with the number of series cells.

An alternative is shielding, where a separate safety mechanism evaluates candidate actions using simplified predictive checks and overrides unsafe actions with a known safe fallback. Shielding can provide strong safety guarantees during both training and execution when the shield is constructed to conservatively enforce constraints \cite{alshiekh2018shielding}. This perspective motivates lightweight shields that avoid solving a full quadratic program online while still preventing unsafe transitions.

\subsubsection{Residual policy learning over a baseline controller}
Residual RL reduces exploration burden by learning corrections around a baseline controller $\pi_{\text{base}}(s_t)$, such as CCCV or MPC. The applied action is
\begin{equation}
a_t
=
\pi_{\text{base}}(s_t) + \delta_t,
\qquad
\delta_t \sim \pi_{\theta}^{\text{res}}(\cdot \mid s_t),
\label{eq_residual_policy}
\end{equation}
so the agent searches in a smaller neighborhood around a reasonable default, which can accelerate learning and improve stability \cite{johannink2019residual}. In safety critical charging, residual learning pairs naturally with shielding, since the baseline provides a safe backbone and the shield rejects residuals that would violate per cell limits.

The above limitations motivate hybrid designs that combine a strong baseline, safe exploration, and robustness to sensing errors and cell variability.


\subsection{Uncertainty Quantification and Ensemble Methods}
\label{sec_uq_ensembles}

Fast charging is inherently safety critical, and the controller must act on predictions that can be unreliable due to sensing noise, cell variability, aging, and operating points not covered by training data. A useful distinction is between aleatoric uncertainty, which reflects irreducible randomness such as sensor noise, and epistemic uncertainty, which reflects lack of knowledge due to limited or shifted data \cite{kendall2017uncertainties}. In safety constrained control, epistemic uncertainty is particularly important because it can indicate out of distribution conditions where aggressive actions are risky.

\subsubsection{Deep ensembles}
A practical and well calibrated approach to epistemic uncertainty uses deep ensembles \cite{lakshminarayanan2017simple}. Consider an ensemble of $M$ predictors $\{f_m(x)\}_{m=1}^{M}$ trained with different initializations or data shuffles. The ensemble predictive mean is
\begin{equation}
\hat{\mu}(x)=\frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^{M} f_m(x).
\label{eq_deep_ens_mean}
\end{equation}
A simple estimator of epistemic uncertainty is the disagreement variance
\begin{equation}
\hat{\sigma}^2(x)=\frac{1}{M-1}\sum_{m=1}^{M}\bigl(f_m(x)-\hat{\mu}(x)\bigr)^2.
\label{eq_deep_ens_var}
\end{equation}
Large $\hat{\sigma}^2(x)$ indicates low confidence and motivates conservative actions or stronger safety margins.

\subsubsection{Probabilistic ensembles in model based RL}
Ensembles also underpin sample efficient model based RL, where the dynamics are learned and used for planning \cite{chua2018deep}. A common formulation uses an ensemble of probabilistic models that predict a distribution over the next state
\begin{equation}
\hat{p}_m(s_{t+1}\mid s_t,a_t)=\mathcal{N}\bigl(\mu_m(s_t,a_t),\Sigma_m(s_t,a_t)\bigr),
\label{eq_pets_prob_model}
\end{equation}
where $\Sigma_m$ captures aleatoric uncertainty and disagreement across $\mu_m$ captures epistemic uncertainty. Trajectory sampling across models reduces brittleness to model error, though online planning can still be too costly for embedded BMS constraints.

\subsubsection{Gaussian process regression for battery prognostics}
In battery health and aging prediction, Gaussian process regression is widely used because it provides principled predictive intervals \cite{liu2019gaussian}. A Gaussian process prior is
\begin{equation}
f(x)\sim \mathcal{GP}\bigl(m(x),k(x,x')\bigr),
\label{eq_gp_prior}
\end{equation}
and with training targets $y$ and kernel matrix $K$ the predictive mean and variance at $x_\ast$ are
\begin{equation}
\mu(x_\ast)=k_\ast^\top\bigl(K+\sigma_n^2 I\bigr)^{-1}y,
\label{eq_gp_post_mean}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\sigma^2(x_\ast)=k(x_\ast,x_\ast)-k_\ast^\top\bigl(K+\sigma_n^2 I\bigr)^{-1}k_\ast.
\label{eq_gp_post_var}
\end{equation}
Despite their attractive uncertainty properties, standard GP methods scale poorly with dataset size, which limits direct online use when transition data becomes large.

\subsubsection{Quantile based uncertainty}
Distribution free predictive intervals can be obtained via quantile regression \cite{koenker2001quantile}. For a quantile level $\tau\in(0,1)$, the pinball loss is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\tau}(y,\hat{y})=
\begin{cases}
\tau\,(y-\hat{y}), & y\ge \hat{y},\\
(1-\tau)\,(\hat{y}-y), & y<\hat{y}.
\end{cases}
\label{eq_pinball_loss}
\end{equation}
Training a neural predictor to output multiple quantiles yields a prediction interval whose width is an uncertainty proxy. Another scalable approximate Bayesian alternative is Monte Carlo dropout, which interprets dropout at inference time as approximate Bayesian inference \cite{gal2016dropout}.

\subsubsection{Adaptive ensemble weighting}
Many ensembles use static averaging, which is suboptimal when operating conditions change due to temperature shifts, aging, or distribution drift. A simple adaptive weighting rule based on recent error and uncertainty histories can be written as
\begin{equation}
w_{m,t}=\frac{\exp\bigl(-\alpha e_{m,t}-\beta u_{m,t}\bigr)}{\sum_{j=1}^{M}\exp\bigl(-\alpha e_{j,t}-\beta u_{j,t}\bigr)},
\label{eq_softmax_weighting}
\end{equation}
where $e_{m,t}$ is a rolling prediction error metric and $u_{m,t}$ is an uncertainty proxy for model $m$. More generally, mixture of experts style gating learns a context dependent weighting function, often implemented with a small neural gate \cite{shazeer2017outrageously}. Let $z_t$ collect features such as recent errors, uncertainties, and operating point indicators, then
\begin{equation}
w_t=\mathrm{softmax}\bigl(g_{\psi}(z_t)\bigr),
\label{eq_gate_softmax}
\end{equation}
which permits rapid shifts in reliance when one expert becomes unreliable.

\section{Problem Formulation}

\subsection{Motivation}
Fast charging of a lithium ion battery pack is a safety critical control problem. The controller must deliver as much charge as possible in the shortest time while ensuring that every individual cell remains within strict voltage and temperature limits. Even brief excursions beyond these limits accelerate degradation and can trigger unsafe failure modes. The pack setting makes this harder because cell to cell variations in capacity, resistance, and thermal coupling produce unequal voltage responses and unequal heat generation under the same series current. Pack level averages can therefore look safe while an outlier cell is already near a hard limit.

We formulate the task as a constrained Markov decision process defined by the tuple $(\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, P, r, c, \gamma)$. The environment is a high fidelity simulation of a series connected pack with $N=10$ cells. The objective is to move the pack from an initial state of charge in the range $10\%$ to $30\%$ to a target of $80\%$ as quickly as possible while satisfying hard safety constraints for every cell at each discrete time step $t$ with sampling interval $\Delta t = 1\,\mathrm{s}$. Safety is monitored using worst cell quantities such as $V_{\max,t}=\max_i V_{i,t}$ and $T_{\max,t}=\max_i T_{i,t}$ so that no single cell can be hidden by pack averaging.

\subsection{Battery and pack model}
A controller intended for deployment must be evaluated in an environment that reproduces the dynamics that actually constrain fast charging. These include electrochemical polarization, thermal buildup, aging, and heterogeneity. A model that ignores any of these can overestimate achievable charging speed or underestimate risk. We therefore model each cell with an electro thermal equivalent circuit coupled to a semi empirical aging law, then aggregate cells into a physically consistent pack with parameter variation and thermal coupling.

\subsubsection{Single cell electro thermal dynamics}
Each cell is represented by a second order Thevenin model, consisting of an ohmic resistance $R_0$ and two RC polarization pairs $(R_1,C_1)$ and $(R_2,C_2)$. This choice balances fidelity and computational tractability. The ohmic term captures the instantaneous voltage drop that determines proximity to the voltage limit during high current charging. The RC terms capture charge transfer and diffusion polarization which create the delayed voltage response that becomes critical near the end of charge.

State of charge evolves via Coulomb counting
\begin{equation}
\frac{d z_i}{dt} = -\frac{\eta\,I}{Q_{\mathrm{eff},i}\,3600},
\end{equation}
where $\eta = 0.995$ is Coulombic efficiency, $I$ is the series current shared by all cells, and $Q_{\mathrm{eff},i}$ is the effective capacity that decreases with aging. This equation is the standard BMS backbone, so learning on top of it encourages deployment realism.

The polarization voltages $v_{1,i}$ and $v_{2,i}$ follow
\begin{equation}
\frac{d v_{k,i}}{dt} = -\frac{v_{k,i}}{R_{k,i} C_{k,i}} + \frac{I}{C_{k,i}},
\qquad
k \in \{1,2\},
\end{equation}
which captures the fact that polarization builds under current and relaxes when current is reduced. This is precisely the mechanism that a controller exploits when tapering current near voltage limits.

Terminal voltage is computed as
\begin{equation}
V_{i} = V_{\mathrm{ocv}}(z_i) - I\,R_{0,i}^{\mathrm{eff}} - v_{1,i} - v_{2,i},
\end{equation}
where $V_{\mathrm{ocv}}(z_i)$ is interpolated from a manufacturer lookup table spanning $3.0\,\mathrm{V}$ at $0\%$ SoC to $4.2\,\mathrm{V}$ at $100\%$ SoC. The effective ohmic resistance
\begin{equation}
R_{0,i}^{\mathrm{eff}} = R_{0,i} + R_{0,i}^{\mathrm{growth}}
\end{equation}
includes aging induced growth. This coupling is essential because an aged cell experiences a larger voltage rise under the same current, which directly tightens the feasible charging envelope.

Temperature follows a lumped thermal model
\begin{equation}
\frac{dT_i}{dt} = \frac{Q_{\mathrm{gen},i} - Q_{\mathrm{diss},i}}{C_{\mathrm{th}}},
\qquad
Q_{\mathrm{gen},i} = I^2 R_{0,i}^{\mathrm{eff}},
\qquad
Q_{\mathrm{diss},i} = hA\,(T_i - T_{\mathrm{amb}}),
\end{equation}
with $C_{\mathrm{th}} = 75\,\mathrm{J/^{\circ}C}$ and $hA = 0.5\,\mathrm{W/^{\circ}C}$. The dominant heat source during high rate charging is resistive heating, so this model captures the main thermal driver while remaining fast enough for repeated rollouts during training and shielding.

\subsubsection{Aging model and parameter coupling}
Fast charging that ignores aging can look optimal in a single episode yet produce unacceptable life loss over repeated cycles. We therefore include an aging model that updates both capacity and resistance to create a realistic feedback loop. Capacity fade reduces usable charge acceptance. Resistance growth increases heating and accelerates future aging.

We model loss of capacity through a stress factor law
\begin{equation}
\frac{dQ_{\mathrm{loss},i}}{dt} = k_1\,S_{I,i}\,S_{T,i}\,S_{z,i},
\end{equation}
where the stress factors represent current, temperature, and state of charge effects
\begin{equation}
S_{I,i} = \frac{|I|}{Q_{\mathrm{nom},i}},
\qquad
S_{T,i} = \exp\!\big(k_2 (T_i - T_{\mathrm{ref}})\big),
\qquad
S_{z,i} = \exp\!\big(k_3 z_i\big),
\end{equation}
with $k_2 = 0.05$, $T_{\mathrm{ref}}=25\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$, and $k_3 = 0.1$. These forms reflect well known qualitative trends. Higher C rate increases plating and mechanical stress. Higher temperature accelerates side reactions. High SoC increases parasitic oxidation and electrolyte decomposition.

We map accumulated loss to a normalized fade variable
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{fade}_i = \min\!\left(\frac{Q_{\mathrm{loss},i}}{0.2 Q_{\mathrm{nom},i}},\,0.8\right),
\end{equation}
then update effective parameters
\begin{equation}
Q_{\mathrm{eff},i} = Q_{\mathrm{nom},i}(1-\mathrm{fade}_i),
\qquad
R_{0,i}^{\mathrm{growth}} = 2 R_{0,i}\,\mathrm{fade}_i.
\end{equation}
This ensures that aging changes both SoC dynamics and thermal dynamics, which forces the controller to consider long term stress rather than only short term progress.

\subsubsection{Pack heterogeneity and thermal coupling}
Real packs are heterogeneous. Cells with slightly lower capacity or higher resistance reach voltage limits earlier and heat faster, which often dictates the pack level limit. If training assumes identical cells, the learned policy can be brittle when deployed. We therefore inject realistic variation through correlated sampling
\begin{equation}
Q_{\mathrm{nom},i} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(2.5\,\mathrm{Ah},\,5\%\right),
\qquad
R_{0,i} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(20\,\mathrm{m\Omega},\,20\%\right),
\end{equation}
with correlation $\rho_{QR}=-0.3$ so that lower capacity tends to co occur with higher resistance. Initial SoC is perturbed as
\begin{equation}
z_{i,0} = z_{0} + \xi_i,
\qquad
\xi_i \sim \mathcal{U}(-0.05,\,0.05),
\end{equation}
which creates imbalance from the start, as is typical after storage or prior usage. Thermal coupling between adjacent cells is modeled through nearest neighbor conduction, producing spatial temperature gradients that affect both safety and aging.

\subsubsection{BMS realism through sensor noise and estimation}
Controllers do not have access to perfect states. Voltage and temperature sensors exhibit noise, and SoC is estimated rather than measured. To avoid learning policies that exploit simulator access, we corrupt measurements with Gaussian noise
\begin{equation}
\tilde V_{i,t} = V_{i,t} + \nu^V_{i,t},
\qquad
\nu^V_{i,t} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_V^2),
\qquad
\sigma_V = 10\,\mathrm{mV},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\tilde T_{i,t} = T_{i,t} + \nu^T_{i,t},
\qquad
\nu^T_{i,t} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_T^2),
\qquad
\sigma_T = 0.5\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}.
\end{equation}
SoC estimation follows noisy Coulomb counting
\begin{equation}
\hat{z}_{i,t+1} = \mathrm{clip}\!\left(\hat{z}_{i,t} - \eta_i\frac{I_t \Delta t}{C_i\,3600} + \epsilon_t,\,0,\,1\right),
\qquad
\epsilon_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_{\mathrm{soc}}^2),
\qquad
\sigma_{\mathrm{soc}} = 0.01.
\end{equation}
This forces the learned controller to remain robust to estimation error, which is a practical deployment requirement.

\subsection{Constrained Markov Decision Process(CMDP) specification}

\subsubsection{State space}
The observed state $s_t$ must be informative enough for safe decisions while remaining measurable in a real BMS. We therefore construct $s_t$ from noisy estimates and worst cell summaries. The state includes progress, safety margins, imbalance, and recent actuation history, plus uncertainty features produced by the predictive ensemble
\begin{equation}
s_t =
\Big[
\bar z_t,\,
V_{\min,t},\,
V_{\max,t},\,
T_t,\,
\sigma_{z,t},\,
I_{t-1},\,
I_{t-1}^2,\,
u^{\mathrm{gru}}_t,\,
u^{\mathrm{mlp}}_t,\,
u^{\mathrm{rf}}_t
\Big].
\end{equation}
Here $V_{\min,t}=\min_i \tilde V_{i,t}$ and $V_{\max,t}=\max_i \tilde V_{i,t}$ provide early warning of voltage saturation. The term $I_{t-1}^2$ is included because resistive heating scales with current squared, so it provides a direct proxy for imminent thermal rise. All continuous features are normalized to $[0,1]$ using running statistics collected during training. A sliding window of the last $L=20$ steps is used as policy input to capture thermal inertia and polarization memory.

\subsubsection{Action space}
The action is a normalized current command $a_t \in [-1,1]$ mapped to physical current as
\begin{equation}
I_t = -\frac{1}{2} I_{\max}\,(a_t + 1).
\end{equation}
With this convention, $a_t=-1$ yields $I_t=0$ as a safe fallback, while $a_t=1$ yields $I_t=-I_{\max}$ as maximum charging current. We set $I_{\max}$ to correspond to a $5C$ limit, consistent with aggressive fast charging scenarios. Actions are held constant over each $1\,\mathrm{s}$ step, consistent with typical BMS control update rates.

\subsubsection{Reward design}
Fast charging is inherently multi objective. Higher current reduces time but increases heating, imbalance growth, and aging. The reward must therefore encode a practical trade off while remaining dense enough for stable learning. We use
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
r_t =&\; 10\,\bar z_t
- 0.01
- 0.1\max(0,T_t - 40)^2
- 0.05\,(1000\,\Delta V_t)
- 1.0\,n_{\mathrm{safety},t}
+ 50\,\mathbf{1}\big[\bar z_t \ge 0.8\big],
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta V_t = V_{\max,t} - V_{\min,t}$ is voltage spread and $n_{\mathrm{safety},t}$ counts the number of per cell violations at time $t$. The time penalty assigns a cost to each step so shorter episodes are preferred. The quadratic temperature penalty reflects the nonlinear increase of degradation risk with temperature, so the controller is discouraged from hovering near thermal limits. The imbalance term encourages keeping cells aligned, which improves both safety margin and charge acceptance for the worst cell. The completion bonus stabilizes the objective by explicitly rewarding reaching the target SoC.

\subsubsection{Hard safety constraints, cost, and termination}
Hard limits must be satisfied for every cell at every time step
\begin{equation}
V_{\min} \le V_{i,t} \le V_{\max},
\qquad
T_{i,t} \le T_{\max},
\qquad
\forall i,\forall t.
\end{equation}
These constraints represent non negotiable safety requirements rather than soft preferences. We therefore introduce a safety cost
\begin{equation}
c_t = \mathbf{1}\!\Big[V_{\max,t} > V_{\max}\ \mathrm{or}\ T_{\max,t} > T_{\max}\Big],
\end{equation}
and require the expected cumulative cost to be zero under the deployed policy. Episodes terminate when $\bar z_t \ge 0.8$. Episodes also terminate under critical violations, such as pack level over voltage or under voltage, or sustained thermal exceedance. This separation is useful in practice because it allows transient penalties to shape learning while still enforcing strict termination for conditions that would trigger a real system shutdown.

\section{Methodology: Safe Adaptive Ensemble Reinforcement Learning(SAERL)}

\subsection{Design rationale}
A pure reinforcement learning controller trained by trial and error is unsuitable for this problem because unsafe exploration is unacceptable. A pure model based controller such as MPC provides structure and constraint handling, but its performance degrades under model mismatch, aging drift, and unmodeled heterogeneity. It can also become computationally expensive as pack complexity increases. These limitations motivate a hybrid design that uses model based control as a safe starting point while learning targeted improvements and adapting to uncertainty online.

Safe Adaptive Ensemble Reinforcement Learning integrates four modules. A predictive ensemble provides short horizon forecasts with uncertainty. An adaptive gating network fuses experts based on recent accuracy and current uncertainty. A residual policy learns corrections to an MPC baseline action. A safety shield filters candidate actions through a one step physics rollout to guarantee feasibility.

\subsection{Predictive ensemble and uncertainty}
Point predictions alone are risky because they do not tell the controller when it should be cautious. We therefore use a diverse ensemble to capture different inductive biases and to expose epistemic uncertainty through disagreement.

The ensemble contains three predictors. A GRU with quantile outputs captures temporal dependencies in polarization and temperature. An MLP with quantile outputs captures nonlinear feature interactions without relying on recurrent memory. A random forest regressor adds robustness to outliers and provides variance across trees as a nonparametric uncertainty estimate.

Each model predicts next step targets that directly affect safety and control, namely $\bar z_{t+1}$, $V_{\min,t+1}$, $T_{t+1}$, and $\Delta V_{t+1}$. For the GRU and MLP, we train quantiles $\tau \in \{0.1,0.5,0.9\}$ using the pinball loss
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_\tau(y,\hat y)=
\begin{cases}
\tau (y-\hat y), & y \ge \hat y,\\
(1-\tau)(\hat y-y), & y < \hat y.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
The prediction interval width
\begin{equation}
u^{m}_t = q^{m}_{0.9}(t) - q^{m}_{0.1}(t)
\end{equation}
acts as an uncertainty proxy. It grows when dynamics become harder to predict, such as near voltage saturation, under high C rate, or under shifting resistance due to aging. For the random forest, we compute $\hat\mu$ and $\hat\sigma$ from tree predictions and set
\begin{equation}
q^{\mathrm{rf}}_{0.1} = \hat\mu - 1.64\hat\sigma,
\qquad
q^{\mathrm{rf}}_{0.9} = \hat\mu + 1.64\hat\sigma,
\qquad
u^{\mathrm{rf}}_t = q^{\mathrm{rf}}_{0.9} - q^{\mathrm{rf}}_{0.1}.
\end{equation}
This gives all experts a comparable uncertainty representation.

\subsection{Adaptive gating network}
A static average of expert predictions is suboptimal because each model dominates in different regimes. The GRU may excel during smooth charging trajectories. The random forest may be more reliable under noisy measurements. Aging drift can also change the relative ranking of experts over time. We therefore learn a gate that allocates trust online based on context, recent error, and uncertainty.

Let $\hat y_{m,t}$ be expert $m$ prediction and $u_{m,t}$ its uncertainty proxy. We maintain a rolling mean absolute error estimate over the last $K=10$ steps
\begin{equation}
e_{m,t} = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=0}^{K-1} \left|y_{t-k} - \hat y_{m,t-k}\right|.
\end{equation}
The gate takes as input the current state features, the current candidate action, the vector of errors $(e_{m,t})$, and uncertainties $(u_{m,t})$. A two layer MLP outputs logits $\ell_{m,t}$ and weights are obtained by softmax
\begin{equation}
w_{m,t} = \frac{\exp(\ell_{m,t})}{\sum_j \exp(\ell_{j,t})},
\qquad
w_{m,t} \ge 0,
\qquad
\sum_m w_{m,t}=1.
\end{equation}
The fused prediction and fused uncertainty are
\begin{equation}
\hat y_t = \sum_m w_{m,t}\,\hat y_{m,t},
\qquad
\hat u_t = \sum_m w_{m,t}\,u_{m,t}.
\end{equation}
Weights are refreshed every 50 steps to reduce compute overhead, which is important for real time feasibility.

\subsection{Residual policy over an MPC baseline}
Learning a full charging policy from scratch requires extensive exploration, which is unsafe and sample inefficient. We therefore learn a residual correction to an MPC baseline action. The baseline provides a conservative, model informed default. The residual focuses learning on the gap between baseline behavior and the best achievable charging speed under the true environment.

Let $a_{\mathrm{mpc},t}$ be the normalized MPC action. The policy outputs a residual $\delta_t$ and composes
\begin{equation}
a_{\mathrm{raw},t} = \mathrm{clip}\!\left(a_{\mathrm{mpc},t} + \delta_t,\,-1,\,1\right),
\qquad
\delta_t \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid s_t).
\end{equation}
The residual policy $\pi_\theta$ is Gaussian with mean produced by a two layer MLP of width 128 and a learned standard deviation. The input includes the flattened sliding window of the last $L$ feature vectors, plus $a_{\mathrm{mpc},t}$ and the SoC gap to target. This gives the policy both temporal context and the baseline suggestion, which reduces variance and accelerates learning.

\subsection{Safety shield using one step physics rollout}
Even with residual learning, a stochastic policy can propose unsafe actions, especially under distribution shift or early in training. Penalizing violations in the reward is insufficient because it allows violations to occur. We therefore enforce safety through a shield that filters actions before they are applied.

\subsubsection{Candidate generation}
Given $a_{\mathrm{raw},t}$, the shield generates a finite candidate set around the proposed residual. We include 9 linearly spaced residual perturbations within $\pm 0.35$ of the sampled residual, the baseline MPC action as an anchor, a CCCV anchor, and the maximum action $a=1$ so that full rate is always considered when safe. Each candidate $a$ is mapped to physical current using the same affine transform as the environment.

\subsubsection{One step feasibility check}
For each candidate, the shield performs a one step rollout of the full physics model. The complete pack state is saved, the candidate current is applied for one step, constraints are checked, and the original state is restored. A candidate is admissible only if all per cell constraints are satisfied at $t+1$
\begin{equation}
\hat V_{i,t+1} \le V_{\max},
\qquad
\hat T_{i,t+1} \le T_{\max},
\qquad
\hat z_{i,t+1} \le 1,
\qquad
\forall i,
\end{equation}
together with pack level limits and a voltage spread margin of $95\,\mathrm{mV}$ to avoid operating too close to the boundary under sensor noise. This check turns safety into a verified property of the applied action rather than a learned preference.

\subsubsection{Cost based ranking of safe candidates}
When multiple candidates are safe, the shield selects the one that best balances speed and stress using a cost score that mirrors the intended trade offs. For a candidate action $a$, define predicted SoC gain $\Delta \widehat{\mathrm{SoC}}(a)$, predicted temperature excess $\Delta \hat T^+(a)=\max(0,\hat T(a)-T_{\mathrm{soft}})$, and predicted imbalance change $\Delta \hat V_{\mathrm{imb}}(a)$. We also define a degradation proxy consistent with the stress factor structure
\begin{equation}
D_{\mathrm{proxy}}(a) = \mathrm{Cr}(a)^2
\exp\!\left(\frac{\max(0,\hat T(a)-25)}{18}\right)
\exp\!\left(0.8\,\hat z(a)\right),
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{Cr}(a)$ is the C rate magnitude implied by $a$. This proxy penalizes high current at high temperature and high SoC, which are the regimes most associated with accelerated aging.

The score is
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{score}(a) =& -120\,\Delta \widehat{\mathrm{SoC}}(a)
+ \frac{1}{\max(\mathrm{Cr}(a),0.05)}
+ 30\,(\Delta \hat T^+(a))^2
+ 15\,D_{\mathrm{proxy}}(a) \\
&+ 0.005\,\left|\Delta \hat V_{\mathrm{imb}}(a)\right|
+ 300\,\mathbf{1}_{\mathrm{unsafe}}(a)
+ \lambda_u\,\hat u_t,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{1}_{\mathrm{unsafe}}(a)$ is one if the candidate fails feasibility and zero otherwise. The negative SoC gain term promotes fast progress. The inverse C rate term penalizes overly small currents that slow charging. The temperature and degradation terms discourage solutions that are fast but destructive. The uncertainty term makes the selection more conservative when the ensemble is less confident.

The shield selects the admissible candidate with lowest score.

\subsubsection{Fallback and anti stall}
If no candidate is admissible, the shield performs a least stress fallback by scanning from zero current upward toward the baseline until a safe action is found. If none is safe, it applies zero current. This guarantees a safe output for all states.

To avoid persistent near zero behavior when conditions are safe, we add an anti stall rule. When the SoC gap exceeds $0.25$ and a normalized risk measure remains below $0.35$ for 120 consecutive steps, we propose a minimum floor current equal to $\max(0.2\,\mathrm{A},\,0.20 I_{\max})$ and apply it only if it passes the feasibility check. This preserves safety while preventing unnecessary stagnation.

\subsection{Training procedure}
Training is performed in two phases to maximize sample efficiency and reduce unsafe exploration.

In phase one, offline warm start, we generate a dataset of transitions by simulating three behavior policies across diverse pack initializations. These include pure MPC, MPC with small perturbations to explore nearby actions, and CCCV. We also run cross entropy method planning on a subset of scenarios to obtain near optimal short horizon action sequences, then form residual targets $\delta^\ast = a_{\mathrm{cem}} - a_{\mathrm{mpc}}$. The quantile GRU and quantile MLP are trained using pinball loss with early stopping. The random forest is trained by standard regression. The gate is trained to minimize the prediction error of the fused output. The residual policy is initialized by behavioral cloning on $\delta^\ast$.

In phase two, online fine tuning, we optimize the residual policy using proximal policy optimization. During data collection, the shield filters every action so that rollouts remain within the safe region of the state space. Policy updates use the clipped surrogate objective
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}^{\mathrm{clip}}(\theta) =
\hat{\mathbb{E}}_t\left[
\min\!\left(
\rho_t(\theta)\hat A_t,\,
\mathrm{clip}(\rho_t(\theta),1-\epsilon,1+\epsilon)\hat A_t
\right)
\right],
\end{equation}
where $\rho_t(\theta)$ is the likelihood ratio between new and old policies and $\hat A_t$ is the advantage estimate. The gate continues to be updated by supervised prediction loss, with gradients from the RL objective stopped so that the gate remains an accuracy driven fusion module rather than drifting to satisfy the policy loss.

This training structure combines the stability and safety of model based control with the adaptability of reinforcement learning, while the ensemble and shield together provide uncertainty awareness and constraint guarantees under noise, heterogeneity, and aging.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:saerl_mid} summarizes the online decision process executed at every control step. Starting from the noisy BMS observation, SAERL first produces short horizon state forecasts and uncertainty estimates using a predictive ensemble, then adaptively fuses these experts through a gating network informed by recent prediction errors. A residual policy proposes a correction to the baseline MPC action, after which a safety shield verifies a finite set of candidate actions via a one step physics rollout and selects the best feasible action using the shield score and a conservative fallback when needed. Finally, an anti stall rule prevents unnecessarily low charging rates under low risk conditions, and rolling error statistics are updated for the next step.


\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{SAERL online control loop}
\label{alg:saerl_mid}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require observation $s_t$, baseline action $a_{\mathrm{mpc},t}$, rolling errors $\{e_{m,t}\}$, low risk counter $n_{\mathrm{low}}$
\Ensure applied action $a_t$, updated $\{e_{m,t+1}\}$, updated $n_{\mathrm{low}}$

\State $(\hat y_{m,t},u_{m,t}) \gets \textsc{EnsemblePredict}(s_t)\ \forall m$
\State $w_t \gets \textsc{GateWeights}(s_t,a_{\mathrm{mpc},t},\{e_{m,t}\},\{u_{m,t}\})$
\State $\hat u_t \gets \sum_m w_{m,t}u_{m,t}$

\State $\delta_t \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot\mid s_t,a_{\mathrm{mpc},t})$
\State $a_{\mathrm{raw},t} \gets \mathrm{clip}(a_{\mathrm{mpc},t}+\delta_t,-1,1)$

\State $\mathcal{A}_t \gets \textsc{MakeCandidates}(a_{\mathrm{raw},t},a_{\mathrm{mpc},t})$
\State $\mathcal{F} \gets \emptyset$
\ForAll{$a \in \mathcal{A}_t$}
    \State $(\mathrm{ok},\hat x_{t+1}) \gets \textsc{OneStepCheck}(a)$
    \If{$\mathrm{ok}$}
        \State $s(a) \gets \textsc{ShieldScore}(a,\hat x_{t+1},\hat u_t)$
        \State $\mathcal{F} \gets \mathcal{F} \cup \{(a,s(a))\}$
    \EndIf
\EndFor

\If{$\mathcal{F}\neq\emptyset$}
    \State $a^\star_t \gets \arg\min_{(a,s)\in\mathcal{F}} s$
\Else
    \State $a^\star_t \gets \textsc{LeastStressFallback}(a_{\mathrm{mpc},t})$
\EndIf

\State $(a_t,n_{\mathrm{low}}) \gets \textsc{AntiStall}(a^\star_t,\hat u_t,n_{\mathrm{low}},s_t)$
\State Apply $a_t$ and observe realized target $y_{t+1}$
\State $\{e_{m,t+1}\} \gets \textsc{UpdateErrors}(\{e_{m,t}\},y_{t+1},\{\hat y_{m,t}\})$

\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}


\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:experimental_setup}

\subsection{Battery Pack Simulation Environment}
\label{subsec:sim_env}
We evaluate SAERL in a series connected pack simulator with $N=10$ cells. Each cell uses a second order Thevenin ECM with lumped thermal dynamics and an aging law. This environment is used for offline data generation, policy training, and evaluation.

\subsubsection{Per cell dynamics}
\label{subsubsec:cell_dynamics}
For each cell $i \in \{1,\dots,N\}$, SoC $z_i(t)$ evolves as
\begin{equation}
\frac{d z_i}{d t} \;=\; -\frac{\eta\, I(t)}{3600\, Q_{\mathrm{eff},i}(t)},
\qquad \eta = 0.995 .
\end{equation}
Polarization states $v_{k,i}(t)$ follow two RC pairs
\begin{equation}
\frac{d v_{k,i}}{d t} \;=\; -\frac{1}{R_{k,i} C_{k,i}} v_{k,i}(t) \;+\; \frac{1}{C_{k,i}} I(t),
\qquad k \in \{1,2\}.
\end{equation}
Terminal voltage is
\begin{equation}
V_i(t) \;=\; V_{\mathrm{ocv}}\!\big(z_i(t)\big) \;-\; I(t)\, R^{\mathrm{eff}}_{0,i}(t) \;-\; v_{1,i}(t) \;-\; v_{2,i}(t),
\end{equation}
with resistance growth
\begin{equation}
R^{\mathrm{eff}}_{0,i}(t) \;=\; R_{0,i} \;+\; R^{\mathrm{growth}}_{0,i}(t).
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Thermal model}
\label{subsubsec:thermal_model}
Each cell temperature $T_i(t)$ evolves as
\begin{equation}
\frac{d T_i}{d t} \;=\; \frac{Q_{\mathrm{gen},i}(t) \;-\; Q_{\mathrm{diss},i}(t) \;+\; Q^{\mathrm{cond}}_{i}(t)}{C_{\mathrm{th}}}.
\end{equation}
We use
\begin{equation}
Q_{\mathrm{gen},i}(t) \;=\; I(t)^2\, R^{\mathrm{eff}}_{0,i}(t),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
Q_{\mathrm{diss},i}(t) \;=\; hA \big(T_i(t) - T_{\mathrm{amb}}\big),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
Q^{\mathrm{cond}}_{i}(t) \;=\; k_{\mathrm{nn}} \Big( \mathbb{I}_{i>1}\big(T_{i-1}(t)-T_i(t)\big) \;+\; \mathbb{I}_{i<N}\big(T_{i+1}(t)-T_i(t)\big) \Big),
\end{equation}
with $C_{\mathrm{th}} = 75\,\mathrm{J}/^\circ\mathrm{C}$ and $hA = 0.5\,\mathrm{W}/^\circ\mathrm{C}$.

\subsubsection{Aging law}
\label{subsubsec:aging_law}
Capacity loss for each cell follows
\begin{equation}
\frac{d Q_{\mathrm{loss},i}}{d t} \;=\; k_1\, S_{I,i}(t)\, S_{T,i}(t)\, S_{z,i}(t).
\end{equation}
Stress terms are
\begin{equation}
S_{I,i}(t) \;=\; \frac{|I(t)|}{Q_{\mathrm{nom},i}},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
S_{T,i}(t) \;=\; \exp\!\Big(k_2\big(T_i(t)-T_{\mathrm{ref}}\big)\Big),
\qquad k_2 = 0.05,\quad T_{\mathrm{ref}} = 25^\circ\mathrm{C},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
S_{z,i}(t) \;=\; \exp\!\big(k_3 z_i(t)\big),
\qquad k_3 = 0.1.
\end{equation}
Fade, effective capacity, and resistance growth are
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{fade}_i(t) \;=\; \min\!\Big( \frac{Q_{\mathrm{loss},i}(t)}{0.2\, Q_{\mathrm{nom},i}},\, 0.8 \Big),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
Q_{\mathrm{eff},i}(t) \;=\; Q_{\mathrm{nom},i}\big(1-\mathrm{fade}_i(t)\big),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
R^{\mathrm{growth}}_{0,i}(t) \;=\; 2 R_{0,i}\, \mathrm{fade}_i(t).
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Heterogeneity and measurement model}
\label{subsubsec:heterogeneity_measurements}
Cell parameters are sampled with correlation $\rho_{QR}=-0.3$ and marginals
\begin{equation}
Q_{\mathrm{nom},i} \sim \mathcal{N}\big(2.5\,\mathrm{Ah},\, (0.05 \cdot 2.5\,\mathrm{Ah})^2\big),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
R_{0,i} \sim \mathcal{N}\big(20\,\mathrm{m}\Omega,\, (0.20 \cdot 20\,\mathrm{m}\Omega)^2\big).
\end{equation}
Initial SoC imbalance is
\begin{equation}
z_{i,0} \;=\; z_0 \;+\; \xi_i,
\qquad \xi_i \sim \mathcal{U}(-0.05,\, 0.05).
\end{equation}
Measurements are corrupted as
\begin{equation}
\tilde V_{i,t} \;=\; V_{i,t} \;+\; \nu^{V}_{i,t},
\qquad \nu^{V}_{i,t} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_V^2),
\qquad \sigma_V = 10\,\mathrm{mV},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\tilde T_{i,t} \;=\; T_{i,t} \;+\; \nu^{T}_{i,t},
\qquad \nu^{T}_{i,t} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_T^2),
\qquad \sigma_T = 0.5^\circ\mathrm{C}.
\end{equation}
SoC estimation uses noisy Coulomb counting without resets
\begin{equation}
\hat z_{i,t+1} \;=\; \mathrm{clip}\!\Big( \hat z_{i,t} \;-\; \frac{\eta_i I_t \Delta t}{3600\, C_i} \;+\; \epsilon_t,\; 0,\; 1 \Big),
\qquad \epsilon_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_{\mathrm{soc}}^2),
\qquad \sigma_{\mathrm{soc}} = 0.01.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Fast Charging Scenarios}
\label{subsec:scenarios}
We construct $15$ scenarios from NASA Ames \cite{saha2008battery}, CALCE \cite{xing2014battery}, and MATR.io \cite{severson2019data}. Each scenario specifies initial SoC in $[0.10,0.30]$, target SoC $0.80$, and ambient temperature in $[15^\circ\mathrm{C},35^\circ\mathrm{C}]$. Estimator initialization error is sampled uniformly in $[-0.05,0.05]$ SoC. All scenarios are mapped to a common cell model via parameter identification.

\subsection{Baselines and Evaluation Protocol}
\label{subsec:baselines_protocol}
We compare SAERL with CCCV, MPC \cite{yan2020mpc}, PPO only, and Static ensemble plus PPO. Each scenario is repeated $5$ times with different random seeds and metrics are averaged.

A run is accepted if it reaches target within $120$ minutes, satisfies the short duration violation rule, and terminates within $\pm 2\%$ SoC of the target. Let $t_{80}$ be the time to reach $0.80$ SoC and $\mathrm{SoC}_T$ the terminal SoC. Define
\begin{equation}
D_V \;\triangleq\; \max_k \big( t^{\mathrm{end}}_{V,k} - t^{\mathrm{start}}_{V,k} \big),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
D_T \;\triangleq\; \max_k \big( t^{\mathrm{end}}_{T,k} - t^{\mathrm{start}}_{T,k} \big).
\end{equation}
The acceptance indicator is
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{I}_{\mathrm{acc}}
\;\triangleq\;
\mathbb{I}\!\big(t_{80} \le 120\,\mathrm{min}\big)\,
\mathbb{I}\!\big(\max(D_V,D_T) \le 0.05\,\mathrm{s}\big)\,
\mathbb{I}\!\big(|\mathrm{SoC}_T - 0.80| \le 0.02\big).
\end{equation}
Safety events use worst case monitoring with $V_{\max}=4.2\,\mathrm{V}$ and $T_{\max}=45^\circ\mathrm{C}$
\begin{equation}
V_{\max,t} \;=\; \max_{i \in \{1,\dots,N\}} V_{i,t},
\qquad
T_{\max,t} \;=\; \max_{i \in \{1,\dots,N\}} T_{i,t},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{I}_{\mathrm{event}}(t)
\;=\;
\mathbb{I}\!\big(V_{\max,t} > V_{\max}\big) \;+\; \mathbb{I}\!\big(T_{\max,t} > T_{\max}\big),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
N_{\mathrm{events}} \;=\; \sum_{t=0}^{T} \mathbb{I}_{\mathrm{event}}(t).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Training and Hyperparameters}
\label{subsec:training_hparams}
Offline data uses MPC, perturbed MPC, and CCCV rollouts totaling about $5 \times 10^5$ transitions. Quantile GRU and quantile MLP are trained for $200$ epochs with early stopping using Adam, learning rate $10^{-3}$, batch size $256$, and quantiles $\tau \in \{0.1,0.5,0.9\}$ with pinball loss \cite{koenker2001quantile}. The random forest uses $150$ trees, minimum leaf size $5$, and maximum depth $20$.

PPO uses $\gamma=0.99$, $\lambda=0.95$, $\epsilon=0.2$, learning rate $3 \times 10^{-4}$, $4$ epochs per update, mini batch size $64$, and entropy coefficient $0.01$ \cite{schulman2017proximal}. Fine tuning runs for $200$ episodes per scenario.

The anti stall current floor is
\begin{equation}
I_{\mathrm{floor}} \;=\; \max\!\big(0.2\,\mathrm{A},\; 0.20\, I_{\max}\big).
\end{equation}

\section{Results}

We evaluate SAERL against four benchmark controllers, namely CCCV, MPC, PPO only, and static ensemble with PPO, on 27 diverse fast-charging scenarios spanning three public dataset families, NASA, CALCE, and MATR, and three charging objectives, fastest, safe, and long-life. All experiments follow the realistic evaluation protocol described in Section~5, including sensor noise, state estimation errors, and explicit cell-to-cell heterogeneity. Each scenario is assessed across five random seeds, and results are reported as averages unless otherwise stated. A scenario is considered accepted only if it reaches 80\% SoC within 120~min, satisfies the short-duration voltage and temperature rule with no violation longer than 50~ms, and terminates within $\pm 2\%$ of the target SoC.

\subsection{Overall Interpretation}

Taken together, these results support a precise positioning of SAERL. First, it demonstrates \textbf{safety dominance} by preserving zero safety events across all 27 scenarios and outperforming all baselines on hard constraint satisfaction. Second, it provides \textbf{performance improvement} in representative successful cases, where time to 80\% SoC is reduced relative to strong baselines. Third, the main \textbf{acceptance bottleneck} is target-reaching performance rather than unsafe behaviour, especially on NASA data, which points to a clear domain-generalization challenge. Fourth, \textbf{component importance} is confirmed through ablations, where the safety shield is indispensable and the adaptive gate with ensemble diversity each contributes meaningfully to success. Finally, \textbf{real-time feasibility} is established through the measured inference latency of 28.7~ms, while a compact version offers a practical trade-off between computational cost and acceptance.

Overall, the framework is already a \textbf{safety-dominant hybrid controller} that preserves zero safety events across all 27 scenarios, slightly improves final SoC and q-loss relative to MPC on aggregate, and clearly outperforms CCCV on safety while reaching the 80\% threshold much earlier. However, the main barrier to higher acceptance remains performance, especially target-reaching under conservative operation and transfer to NASA-style aging scenarios. The present evidence therefore supports SAERL as a robust and practically promising control framework, while also showing that \textbf{improved domain generalization and stronger target-seeking behaviour} are still required before broader acceptance gains can be claimed.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

SAERL's 40\% pass rate, while far from perfect, represents a substantial advance over the 0\% achieved by both CCCV and MPC under the same realistic protocol. The key enablers are threefold. First, the ensemble with adaptive gating provides robust one-step predictions even when individual models are uncertain; the gate's ability to down-weight high-uncertainty predictors prevents the policy from acting on unreliable forecasts. Second, the \textbf{residual formulation} dramatically accelerates learning---the policy only needs to learn corrections to a reasonable baseline, rather than discovering viable charging currents from random initialization. Third, the \textbf{differentiable safety shield} offers a strong inductive bias: the agent is free to explore aggressive actions, but the shield ensures that the environment never experiences unsafe voltages or temperatures. This separation of concerns (optimization vs. constraint satisfaction) is crucial for safe RL in physical systems.

Why does SAERL still fail in 9/15 scenarios? The failures are concentrated in high-ambient-temperature cases where the safety shield, to maintain zero predicted violations, often drives the current to the anti-stall floor ($0.1\,\mathrm{C}$). This prolongs charging and, in three instances, still results in brief overtemperature events when the model's prediction error underestimates thermal accumulation. Improving the ensemble's calibration under thermal stress---perhaps by including explicit temperature rate features---is a clear next step.

Another notable finding is that family-specific tuning eliminates all safety events but at a speed penalty. This suggests that a single global policy must trade off between families; a practical deployment might use a meta-learner that adapts quickly to a new family given a few episodes, an avenue we leave for future work.

The core innovations are:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Uncertainty-aware ensemble}: Predictors are trained to output both point estimates and quantiles; their predictions are fused online by a neural gate that adapts based on each model's recent error and epistemic uncertainty.
    \item \textbf{Residual RL over a safe baseline}: The policy learns corrections to an MPC baseline, accelerating learning while preserving a fallback safety layer that intervenes whenever constraints are threatened.
    \item \textbf{Realistic evaluation protocol}: We simulate continuous state estimation with additive sensor noise and explicitly model cell-to-cell capacity and resistance spreads, moving beyond single-cell, noise-free evaluations.
    \item \textbf{Comprehensive validation}: Using 15 scenarios from three public datasets, SAERL achieves a 40\% acceptance rate (6/15 scenarios) under stringent pass criteria---a substantial improvement over 0\% for both CCCV and MPC. SAERL matches MPC's zero-safety-event capability in 12/15 runs while being 8.6\% faster to 80\% SoC, and reduces safety violations by 97.3\% compared to CCCV.
\end{enumerate}


\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limitations}

Despite promising results, SAERL has several limitations that warrant discussion.

\paragraph{Chemistry and aging generalization.}
The model and policies were developed and validated on a single NMC/graphite chemistry. While the ensemble predictors can be retrained on other chemistries, the safety shield relies on the fused forward model, which may not extrapolate accurately to very different electrochemical behaviors (e.g., LFP's flat OCV curve). We plan to extend SAERL to multiple chemistries via domain randomization and meta-learning.

\paragraph{Long-term degradation.}
Our aging model, while physically motivated, is a semi-empirical approximation. The degradation penalty $\hat{D}_t$ in the reward function is a proxy; we have not validated that SAERL-minimized policies actually extend cycle life in hardware. Collaborative work with experimental groups is underway to test SAERL on physical cells.

\paragraph{Pack-level transfer.}
The current pack model assumes uniform temperature and ignores electrical imbalances beyond SoC variance. Real packs exhibit spatially varying temperatures, aging gradients, and wiring resistances. We are developing a higher-fidelity pack simulator that incorporates these effects and will evaluate SAERL's robustness to such unmodeled dynamics.

\paragraph{Computational overhead.}
Although SAERL's 26\,ms latency is acceptable for a BMS, the ensemble and shield require a modest embedded processor; porting to a fixed-point MCU with limited memory may necessitate further compression (e.g., quantization, distillation). This engineering challenge is left for future work.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented SAERL, a hybrid safe-charging controller that combines uncertainty-aware ensemble prediction, adaptive gating, residual reinforcement learning over an MPC anchor, and a hard safety shield for lithium-ion battery pack fast charging, and showed on 27 benchmark scenarios from NASA, CALCE, and MATR that it was the only evaluated method to achieve non-zero acceptance, passing 7/27 cases (25.9\%) while preserving zero safety failures and zero temperature failures, passing the q-loss criterion in 88.9\% of scenarios, reaching a mean time to 80\% SoC of 73.3\,min, and operating at a mean inference latency of 28.7\,ms; relative to CCCV, SAERL removes a large number of safety events, and relative to MPC it eliminates safety violations while improving final SoC and slightly reducing q-loss, but its main limitation remains performance rather than safety, since most failures arise from insufficient target-reaching progress and are concentrated in the NASA regime where predictive support is weakest, implying that the key next research step is not stricter shielding but better domain generalization, richer health-aware representations, and stronger long-horizon target-seeking under domain shift.

The broader implication for research is that hybrid, safety-filtered reinforcement learning is a credible direction for battery charging, but its future gains will come from better domain generalization, richer health-aware state representations, and stronger long-horizon decision making rather than from adding more conservative safety logic. In other words, this study suggests that the safety problem can be substantially contained by combining physics-based constraints with learned residual adaptation, but the performance and transfer problem remains open. Future work should therefore prioritize improved representation of aging and regime context, better degradation-grounded reward design, and stronger cross-domain adaptation so that hybrid controllers like SAERL can generalize beyond the operating envelopes that are currently easiest to model. Within that framing, SAERL provides both a working baseline and a reproducible benchmark for subsequent research on safe, adaptive, pack-level fast charging.
